# Objection Handling Prompts

Objections are not rejections. They are the prospect telling you what they need to believe before buying. These prompts help you decode what's really being said and respond without sounding defensive or desperate.

---

## 1. Decode the real objection

```
A prospect told me: "[exact quote of the objection]"

Context: [where we are in the deal, who they are, what's happened so far]

1. List the 3 most likely things this actually means, ranked. ("Too expensive" can mean: no budget, no perceived value, no authority, negotiating, or polite brush-off.)
2. For each interpretation, one question I can ask to find out which it is
3. What I should NOT do (the instinctive response that makes it worse)
```

---

## 2. Objection response builder

```
I sell [product] at [price]. My buyer is [role]. The value we deliver: [specific outcome].

Common objection I keep hearing: "[objection]"

Build me a response using this structure:
1. Acknowledge without agreeing (one sentence, no "I completely understand")
2. Reframe: the question behind the question
3. Evidence: how to answer with a story or number, not a claim
4. Check: a question that tests if the objection is resolved

Then give me the 2-sentence version I can actually say on a call without sounding scripted.
```

---

## 3. The objection gym (roleplay)

```
You are a prospect who wants to say no politely. I sell [product] at [price].

Throw objections at me one at a time, starting easy and getting harder:
1. "Send me some information"
2. "We don't have budget this year"
3. "We're already using [competitor]"
4. "I need to think about it"
5. "Your product is too expensive"
6. "We tried something like this before and it didn't work"
7. [a brutal one specific to my product that I won't see coming]

After each of my responses, stay in character and react realistically: push back if I was weak, soften if I was good. At the end, score each response 1-10 and tell me which habit is costing me the most deals.
```

---

## 4. Competitor comparison, without trash talk

```
A prospect asked how we compare to [competitor].

My product: [what it does, key strengths, honest weaknesses]
Their product, as far as I know: [what you know]
This prospect cares about: [what you learned in discovery]

Write a response that:
1. Respects the competitor (trash talk always backfires)
2. Frames the comparison around THIS prospect's priorities, not a feature grid
3. Names one thing the competitor genuinely does well, and why we chose a different trade-off
4. Ends with a question that moves the conversation back to their problem

Keep it under 120 words. Confidence without spin.
```

---

## 5. "Not now" and the stalled deal

```
A deal has stalled. Last contact: [when]. Their last message: "[quote]"

The deal so far: [2-3 sentences of history]

1. Diagnose: is this deal sleeping, dying, or dead? Evidence for your verdict.
2. If sleeping: write a re-engagement message that adds new value (an insight, a relevant change in their world) rather than "just checking in"
3. If dying: write the direct "should we close this out?" message. Counterintuitively, giving them permission to say no often revives deals.
4. Tell me what I did earlier in the deal that caused this stall, so I stop doing it.
```

---

## Field notes

- **Price objections at the end of a deal are usually discovery failures at the start.** They never understood the cost of their problem, so any price feels high.
- **The best response to most objections is a question,** not an answer. You can't argue someone into buying.
- **"I need to think about it" deserves:** "Sure. Usually when someone says that, there's one specific concern behind it. What's yours?" Kind, direct, works.
- **If you hear the same objection in every deal,** it's not an objection problem. It's a positioning problem. Fix the pitch, not the rebuttal.
