# Follow-Up Prompts

80% of deals need 5+ touches. Most founders stop at 2 because following up feels like begging. It isn't, if every touch adds something. These prompts make follow-ups worth reading.

**Banned forever:** "Just checking in", "Bumping this to the top of your inbox", "Circling back", "Any updates?"

---

## 1. The follow-up sequence

```
I sent a cold email to [name], [title] at [company] and got no reply.

The original email said: [paste]

Write a 4-touch follow-up sequence over 3 weeks:
- Touch 1 (day 3): different angle on the same problem, 2 sentences
- Touch 2 (day 7): share something useful with no ask (insight, benchmark, relevant example from their industry)
- Touch 3 (day 14): brief case study - how someone like them got [result], one sentence ask
- Touch 4 (day 21): the breakup email - closing the loop, door open, zero guilt-tripping

Each under 60 words. Each must make sense if it's the first email they actually read. Never reference being ignored.
```

---

## 2. Post-meeting follow-up

```
I just had a [discovery call / demo / pricing call] with [name] at [company].

What happened: [paste notes]
What we agreed: [next step, or "nothing concrete" - be honest]

Write the follow-up email:
1. One line of context (not "great to chat!")
2. Three bullets max: what I heard their situation to be, in their language
3. The agreed next step with date, or a proposed one with a specific date
4. Nothing else. No feature recaps, no attachments unless promised.

If we agreed on nothing concrete, also tell me what that means about this deal and how the email should differ.
```

---

## 3. Reviving a ghost

```
[Name] at [company] went silent after [what happened - demo, proposal, verbal yes].
Silent for: [duration]. Deal value: [amount].

Everything I've sent since: [list briefly]

1. Diagnose the silence: rank likely causes (priorities shifted, champion lost internal battle, chose competitor, just busy)
2. Write 3 different revival messages:
   - The value-add: new insight or relevant development in their space
   - The direct: "has this dropped off your list?" - honest, no passive aggression
   - The takeaway: mention I'm assuming this isn't a fit for now and closing it out
3. Which one fits my situation and why?
```

---

## 4. The breakup email

The most underrated email in sales. Sent well, it gets more replies than any follow-up.

```
Deal context: [who, what happened, how long silent]

Write a breakup email that:
- Genuinely closes the loop, no guilt, no "I've tried reaching you several times"
- Takes responsibility lightly ("seems the timing isn't right" not "you never answered")
- States what they can come back for and when that door makes sense
- Under 50 words
- Would make them feel slightly sorry to lose me, not relieved

Then a version with light humor, in case that fits my relationship with them.
```

---

## 5. Multi-channel escalation plan

```
Email isn't landing with [name], [title] at [company]. Deal stage: [stage].

Available channels: LinkedIn, phone, video message, physical mail, mutual connection [name if any], events.

Build a 2-week multi-channel plan:
- Which channels, in what order, and why for THIS persona
- What each touch says (draft it)
- Where the line is between persistent and creepy for this seniority level
- The trigger to stop entirely
```

---

## Field notes

- **The reply usually comes from touch 3-5.** If you write only one follow-up, write the breakup email. It outperforms everything.
- **Every touch must survive this test:** "if this is the only email they read, does it give value or beg?"
- **Change the angle, not just the date.** Resending the same pitch louder is not a sequence.
- **Track it.** If you don't know your reply rate per touch, you're guessing. A spreadsheet is enough. See [pipeline stages](../templates/sales-pipeline-stages.md).
