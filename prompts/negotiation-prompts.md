# Negotiation Prompts

Founders are terrible negotiators with their own product. You built it, you need the revenue, and the prospect can feel both. These prompts prepare you so you don't improvise your way into a 40% discount.

---

## 1. Negotiation prep sheet

Use before any pricing conversation.

```
I'm entering a pricing negotiation.

My product: [what it is]
List price: [price and structure]
My floor (below this I walk): [number - decide this NOW, not on the call]
Their situation: [company, urgency, alternatives they have, budget signals]
What I know they want: [from discovery]
What I want besides money: [case study, referrals, logo rights, longer term, faster payment]

Build my prep sheet:
1. My BATNA and theirs - who actually needs this deal more, honestly?
2. Three tradeable variables besides price (scope, term, payment timing, support level, start date)
3. For each likely discount request, a counter that trades instead of caves ("yes, if...")
4. Exact words to hold price without sounding rigid
5. The walk-away script, polite and final
```

---

## 2. Discount request response

```
Prospect asked for [X]% off / a price of [amount]. List price is [amount].

Deal context: [size, how badly I need it, their alternatives, timeline]

Give me:
1. What their ask probably signals (testing vs. real constraint vs. procurement ritual)
2. Three responses, ranked:
   - Hold: keep price, add framing on value
   - Trade: "yes, if" - concession for concession (annual prepay, case study, multi-seat, reference call)
   - Structured drop: if I must move, how to do it once, with a reason, and make it final
3. The exact sentences for each. Calm, no apology, no over-explaining.

Never let me say "I'll see what I can do."
```

---

## 3. Pricing page / proposal review

```
Here is my pricing or proposal:
[paste]

The buyer: [role, company size, what they care about]

Review it as a procurement-savvy buyer:
1. Where would you push, and how hard?
2. What's ambiguous enough to exploit later?
3. Does anything signal desperation or over-eagerness?
4. Is value framed in their numbers (time, money, risk) or just my features?
5. Rewrite the weakest section.
```

---

## 4. Procurement and legal gauntlet

```
I'm a founder selling [product] at [price] to a [size] company. Deal is verbally agreed. Now procurement is involved and asking for: [list demands - security review, discount, payment terms, MSA changes, etc.]

1. Which demands are standard ritual vs. real risk to me? Sort them.
2. Where do I push back, where do I concede fast to keep velocity?
3. Payment terms: they want [e.g. net-60]. What do I counter with and how?
4. Draft a short, friendly-but-firm email responding to their list.

Remember I have no legal team and limited time. Optimize for closing without getting hurt.
```

---

## 5. Renewal and expansion

```
Customer [name] has been using [product] for [time]. Contract: [current price/term]. Renewal in [when].

Their usage and results: [what you know]
What I want: [renewal + price increase / expansion / multi-year]

1. Am I positioned to raise price? Evidence for and against.
2. When and how to open the conversation (hint: not 2 weeks before expiry)
3. Frame the increase in the value they got. Draft the opening message.
4. If they push back, what's my give that doesn't touch the new price?
```

---

## Field notes

- **Decide your floor before the call.** A floor decided during the call is not a floor, it's a suggestion.
- **Every discount needs a trade.** Free discounts teach customers that your price is fiction.
- **Silence after your price.** Say the number, stop talking. The one who talks first usually pays.
- **Speed is a concession too.** "Sign this week and I can hold this quarter's pricing" is often stronger than a discount.
- **The deals you walk away from fund the confidence that closes the rest.**
