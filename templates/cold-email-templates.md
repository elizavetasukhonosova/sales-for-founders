# Cold Email Templates

10 frameworks that work, with examples and the reason each one works. Adapt, don't copy verbatim: a template sent as-is is spam with better formatting.

Universal rules first:

- **Under 90 words.** Nobody reads paragraph 3 of a cold email.
- **One idea, one ask.**
- **The first line earns the second line.** No "Hope you're well."
- **Ask for interest, not time.** "Worth exploring?" beats "Do you have 30 minutes Tuesday?"

---

## 1. The observed trigger

Best reply rates of any framework. Requires actual research.

> **subject:** your sdr hiring
>
> Maria, saw you're hiring 3 SDRs this quarter after the Series A.
>
> Most teams at this stage build outbound on founder intuition, then spend a year unlearning it. We help sales leaders skip that year: [Company] booked 40% more meetings in their first quarter with us.
>
> Worth a look while the team is still small?

**Why it works:** the trigger (hiring) proves relevance and timing. The pain (unlearning) is specific to their moment. The number is concrete.

---

## 2. The problem hypothesis

When you know the persona's world but have no specific trigger.

> **subject:** duplicate leads in [CRM]
>
> Anna, most RevOps leads I talk to at 100+ person companies quietly maintain a spreadsheet on the side because the CRM data can't be trusted.
>
> If that's you: we clean and dedupe automatically, and [Customer] cut their reporting prep from 2 days to 2 hours.
>
> Off base, or worth a look?

**Why it works:** "off base?" lowers the cost of replying. Naming a private pain ("quietly maintain a spreadsheet") shows you know their life.

---

## 3. The referral / mutual connection

Highest conversion when available. Never fake it.

> **subject:** [mutual name] suggested I write
>
> Tom, [Mutual] mentioned you're rebuilding the onboarding flow and hitting the same activation issues she had at [Her Company].
>
> We worked with her team on exactly that - activation went from 23% to 41% in two months.
>
> Happy to share what worked, even if it's just a 15-minute pointer. Interested?

**Why it works:** borrowed trust. The offer to help "even if" removes pitch pressure.

---

## 4. The competitor's customer

> **subject:** [competitor] contract
>
> Igor, noticed [Company] runs on [Competitor]. Most teams we meet like it fine until they need [specific thing it does poorly].
>
> That's the one thing we built ours around. [Customer] switched last year and cut [metric] by 30%.
>
> If [specific thing] is ever the bottleneck, worth a conversation?

**Why it works:** doesn't ask them to hate their vendor. Plants a flag on one weakness and leaves the door open.

## 5. The relevant content opener

> **subject:** your comment on pricing
>
> Sofia, your LinkedIn post about usage-based pricing breaking sales comp hit home - we see the same fight in every PLG company we work with.
>
> We built a comp calculator that handles usage-based models. [Customer]'s RevOps team runs on it.
>
> Want the template version? Free, no strings.

**Why it works:** leads with their thinking, not your product. The free give starts a real conversation.

---

## 6. The direct ask

For senior buyers who respect brevity. Risky with everyone else.

> **subject:** 14 days, 2x demos
>
> Pavel, one sentence: we get B2B SaaS companies 2x demo bookings in 14 days or you don't pay.
>
> If that sounds like marketing math, fair - here are 3 customers you can call: [names].
>
> Worth 15 minutes?

**Why it works:** confidence plus proof. The "if that sounds like marketing math" line disarms the exact objection it triggers.

---

## 7. The case study one-liner

> **subject:** how [peer company] did it
>
> Elena, [Peer Company] had the same setup you do - 5 AEs, no SDRs, founder still closing half the deals.
>
> Twelve weeks later the founder was out of daily sales entirely and revenue grew 20%.
>
> Happy to share exactly what they changed. Useful?

**Why it works:** peers are the most persuasive evidence. "Same setup you do" makes it a mirror, not a pitch.

---

## 8. The question-only email

> **subject:** quick one
>
> Marc - when your team misses quota, how do you currently figure out if it's a pipeline problem or a closing problem?
>
> Asking because most sales leaders tell me they honestly can't tell. We built something for exactly that.

**Why it works:** a genuinely hard question creates a genuine pause. Only works if the question is actually good.

---

## 9. The pre-breakup (first email!)

Contrarian: acknowledge the noise directly.

> **subject:** probably not for you
>
> Julia, you likely get 30 of these a day, so the short version:
>
> We do [one thing] for [her role]s. [Customer] got [result]. If that's not a current priority, delete this with a clear conscience.
>
> If it is: worth 15 minutes?

**Why it works:** honesty stands out in a wall of hype. "Delete with a clear conscience" respects the reader, and respect gets replies.

---

## 10. The internal referral request

When you can't reach the decision maker directly.

> **subject:** right person?
>
> Daniel, I'm trying to find who owns [problem area] at [Company] - my guess is it's you or someone on your team.
>
> We help [role]s with [one-sentence value]. If it's not you, would you point me to the right person? If it is you: worth a quick look?

**Why it works:** small, easy ask. People redirect emails far more willingly than they book meetings, and a forwarded intro beats a cold one.
