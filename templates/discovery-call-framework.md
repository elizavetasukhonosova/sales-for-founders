# The 30-Minute Discovery Call Framework

The exact structure. Not a script to read, a skeleton to internalize. After 10 calls it becomes instinct.

**The goal of discovery is a decision, not a demo.** You're deciding if this is a real deal. They're deciding if you understand their problem. Nobody should see a product screen today.

---

## Minute 0-3: Frame the call

Take control kindly. The person who sets the agenda runs the call.

> "Thanks for the time. Here's what I'd suggest: I'll ask about how you handle [problem area] today, you ask me anything, and by the end we'll both know if it makes sense to keep talking. If it doesn't, I'll say so and won't chase you. Sound fair?"

That last sentence does two things: relaxes them and positions you as someone who qualifies, not begs.

**Skip:** long company intros, slide decks, "let me tell you about us." They'll ask if they care.

---

## Minute 3-15: Current state and pain

Work down this ladder. Each level is worth more than the one above.

1. **Facts:** "How do you handle [X] today?" (easy to answer, gets them talking)
2. **Friction:** "What's the most annoying part of that?"
3. **Impact:** "What does that actually cost you - in hours, money, missed deals?"
4. **Emotion:** "How does the team feel about it?" (people buy relief, not features)
5. **Priority:** "Of everything on your plate, where does fixing this rank?"

**Critical moves:**

- After every answer: pause 2 seconds. They fill silences with the truth.
- "Tell me more about that" whenever you hear a number, a sigh, or the word "manually."
- Write down their exact phrases. Their words become your proposal later.

**The question founders always skip:** "What happens if you do nothing for a year?" If the honest answer is "not much," you don't have a deal, and it's better to know now.

---

## Minute 15-22: Process and money

The awkward questions. Skipping them doesn't make the deal easier, it makes the loss later.

- "If you decided to fix this, who besides you would be involved in that decision?"
- "Have you tried to solve this before? What happened?"
- "Is there budget allocated for this, or would it need to be created?"
- "What's your timeline - is this a this-quarter thing or a someday thing?"

You're not interrogating. Tone: a doctor asking about symptoms, curious and neutral.

---

## Minute 22-27: Bridge, don't demo

Only now do you talk, and briefly:

> "Based on what you've said, here's what I heard: [their problem, their words, their numbers]. That's exactly what we do for [similar customer], who saw [result]. I think it's worth a deeper look. Here's what I'd suggest as a next step..."

If it's NOT a fit, say so: "Honestly, I don't think we're the right solution for this. What you actually need is [X]." This costs you nothing (the deal was dead anyway) and builds a reputation that sends you referrals for years.

---

## Minute 27-30: Lock the next step

Never end with "I'll send some info."

> "Next step I'd suggest: [specific thing] with [specific people] on [specific day]. Does Thursday work?"

Book it on the call, calendar invite sent before you hang up. A deal without a scheduled next step is not in your pipeline, it's in your imagination.

---

## After the call: 10-minute debrief

Run your notes through the [call debrief prompt](../prompts/discovery-call-prompts.md#3-call-debrief-and-next-step-email). Send the follow-up email within 2 hours while you're still the most recent thing in their inbox.

## Scorecard

A discovery call succeeded if you can answer all five:

1. What is their problem, in their words?
2. What does it cost them, in numbers?
3. Who decides, and who else is involved?
4. Why now (or why not now)?
5. What is the scheduled next step?

Three or fewer answers = you did a demo-lite chat. Happens to everyone. Book a second discovery call before showing product.
