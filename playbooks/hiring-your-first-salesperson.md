# Hiring Your First Salesperson

The €100K decision founders most often get wrong. I've been the salesperson hired into this situation, managed people hired into it, and watched founders burn a year on the standard mistakes. Here's the map.

---

## When (the honest checklist)

Hire when ALL of these are true:

- [ ] You personally closed 15-20 deals with a **repeatable pattern**: same buyer type, similar pitch, predictable objections
- [ ] You can write down the sales process on two pages and it would actually work in someone else's hands
- [ ] There's more qualified pipeline than you can physically handle (you're losing deals to your own calendar)
- [ ] You have 12+ months of the hire's fully-loaded cost in the bank, because they'll produce nothing for 3-6 months

If you're hiring because "I hate sales and want it off my plate" - stop. That's not a hiring trigger, that's a delegation fantasy, and it produces the classic failure: an expensive AE with no playbook, no pipeline, and no chance. Fix your relationship with sales first ([founder-led sales](./founder-led-sales.md)), then hire from strength.

## Who: the four wrong hires and the right one

**Wrong hire #1: The big-logo AE.** VP-track seller from Salesforce or SAP. Used to warm leads, brand trust, sales engineering support, and a machine around them. At your startup there is no machine. They flounder politely for two quarters.

**Wrong hire #2: The VP of Sales, too early.** VPs build and run teams. You don't have a team. You're paying strategy prices for a job that's 90% execution right now.

**Wrong hire #3: The pure closer.** "Just get me meetings and I'll close." Nobody's getting them meetings. Early-stage sales is 70% generating your own pipeline.

**Wrong hire #4: Your smart friend with no sales experience.** Trainable in theory; in practice you don't have a training system yet, and you'll both discover that in month 4.

**The right profile:** a scrappy full-cycle seller, roughly years 3-7 of their career, who has SOLD AT A STARTUP BEFORE - sourced their own pipeline, closed without a brand behind them, survived ambiguity. Hungry enough to build, experienced enough not to need building. Often a strong SDR-turned-AE from a small company beats a mid-level AE from a famous one.

Two traits to interview for above all: **curiosity** (do they ask good questions, including about your business?) and **resourcefulness** (make them tell you, in detail, how they built pipeline from nothing - the real ones light up at this question, the pretenders go vague).

## The interview that actually predicts

Skip "walk me through your resume." Do these:

1. **Have them sell you their current product.** Full discovery on you, live. You'll see their real skill in 15 minutes: do they ask or pitch?
2. **The pipeline autopsy.** "Take your last closed-won deal: where did it come from, every step, every stall, exact numbers." Real sellers narrate this like a movie. Fakes give you a highlights reel.
3. **A homework task with your actual product:** "Here's our site and a target account. Write the outreach you'd send." You're grading research, judgment, and writing, the actual job.
4. **Reference calls where you ask one question:** "Would you hire them again for a role with no playbook?" Listen for the pause.

## Setting them up (where founders sabotage the hire)

- **Give them your playbook, live.** Everything in this repo, filled in with YOUR data: the pitch that works, the [pipeline definitions](../templates/sales-pipeline-stages.md), the [discovery framework](../templates/discovery-call-framework.md), recordings of your best calls.
- **Sell WITH them for the first month,** not near them. They shadow you, then you shadow them, then they fly. Handoff is a ramp, not a baton toss.
- **Keep taking sales calls yourself.** The founder who fully exits sales at the first hire loses touch with the market exactly when the playbook is still evolving.
- **Compensation, simply:** market base (don't cheap out - underpaid salespeople leave the week they ramp), variable tied to what they control, no cliff-shaped cleverness. A 50/50 base/variable split at target is a fine default for a full-cycle role.
- **Judge the first 90 days on inputs and process** (pipeline built, quality of discovery, speed of learning), not closed revenue - your sales cycle is longer than their probation period. But DO judge: a great hire shows unmistakable signal by day 60. A wrong one shows it too, and the kindest thing for everyone is acting on it fast.

## The uncomfortable summary

Your first sales hire doesn't fix sales. They multiply whatever sales reality you hand them. Hand them a working machine and you get growth. Hand them chaos and you get expensive, slow-motion proof that the chaos was yours.
