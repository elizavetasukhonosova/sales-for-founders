# Your First 10 Customers

Where they actually come from, in order of probability. Spoiler: not ads, not Product Hunt, not "going viral."

The first 10 are a different sport from customers 11-100. They buy you, not the product. The product is half-broken and they know it. What they're really buying: your attention, your speed, and the bet that you understand their problem better than the incumbents do.

---

## The hierarchy (work top to bottom)

### 1. People who already know you

Former colleagues, clients, classmates, that person you helped once. Not because they'll buy out of charity, but because they'll give you the **honest meeting** strangers won't.

Do this exercise before writing a single cold email: list 50 people you know professionally. For each: could they buy, refer, or advise? Message the top 20 individually. Not a launch announcement blast, but:

> "I'm building [X] for [problem]. You dealt with this at [Company] - can I show you what I have and get your honest reaction? Not selling, calibrating."

Half will talk to you. A few will say "wait, I need this." Those are your first customers, and "not selling" was true, which is exactly why it sells.

### 2. Second-degree: the asked-for intro

After every conversation from tier 1, regardless of outcome:

> "Who else do you know that fights with [problem]?"

An introduced meeting converts perhaps 10x a cold one. The mechanics matter: ask for ONE name, not "anyone you know." Specific asks get answers; vague ones get "I'll think about it."

### 3. Communities where your buyers complain

Every niche has its watering holes: Slack groups, subreddits, LinkedIn comment sections, industry forums. Your buyers are there, describing their problems in their own words, for free.

The play is **useful first, visible second.** Answer questions for 2-3 weeks with zero pitching. Then, when relevant: "I'm actually building something for this - happy to show anyone curious." In a community where you have karma, that message lands. As your first post, it gets you banned.

Bonus: their complaint threads are your messaging research. Steal the vocabulary. See it feed straight into the [problem hypothesis template](../templates/cold-email-templates.md#2-the-problem-hypothesis).

### 4. Targeted cold outreach

Now, and only now, cold. Not because cold doesn't work, but because everything above converts better and teaches faster.

Cold for first customers differs from cold at scale: pick 30 dream accounts, research each properly with the [research prompts](../prompts/cold-outreach-prompts.md), and write emails a human would be glad to receive. At this stage you're allowed the honest founder card:

> "I'm the founder. We're early - which means you'd get me personally, the roadmap bends toward your needs, and the price reflects the stage. In exchange I need feedback and, if it works, a reference."

That trade is genuinely good for a certain kind of buyer: the frustrated early adopter. You only need 10 of them in the world.

### 5. Being findable for the desperate

A basic site that says clearly what problem you solve, one honest blog post or comparison page per real question buyers google, a LinkedIn profile that explains your company in the first line. Not a growth strategy yet - just making sure the person actively searching for a fix at 11pm can find you and see signs of life.

## Pricing the first 10

- **Charge from customer one.** Free pilots produce feedback like "it's nice." Paying customers produce feedback like "this is broken and I need it fixed by Friday" - which is the feedback that builds companies. A €99/month pilot beats a free one by an order of magnitude in information quality.
- **Discount for the stage, loudly and temporarily.** "Founding customer pricing, locked for 2 years" rewards the risk they're taking and expires as you mature.
- **Never discount silently.** Every discount buys something: a case study, a reference call, a logo, an intro. Practice with the [negotiation prompts](../prompts/negotiation-prompts.md) - the habits you build on deal 3 are the ones you'll have on deal 300.

## What to obsess over (and what to ignore)

Obsess: **time from signature to their first "wow."** Your first 10 customers churn or evangelize based on the first two weeks. Onboard them personally, over-communicate, fix their bugs first.

Ignore: scalability of any of this. "Does it scale?" is the wrong question for a year. Do things that don't scale until the repeatable pattern reveals itself - then you're ready for the [founder-led sales system](./founder-led-sales.md) to run at full speed.
